Introduction to Cryptography

Cryptography History Ciphers

Cryptography involves studying methods for concealing information and ensuring its integrity through protocols and algorithms designed to prevent unauthorized access. The term derives from Greek: kryptós (hidden) and gráphein (to write). In modern times, cryptography has become foundational to digital security, evolving from linguistic art to an interdisciplinary science relying on discrete mathematics, number theory, information theory, computational complexity, statistics, and combinatorics.

Key roles include cryptographers (who develop systems) and practitioners of cryptanalysis or codebreaking (who attempt to bypass protections). The broader field is cryptology, encompassing both creation and breaking of systems.

Distinction from steganography: While cryptography obscures message content, steganography hides message existence entirely (microdots, invisible ink, hidden image data).

Plaintext, Ciphertext, and Ciphers

Encryption transforms readable information (plaintext) into unreadable form (ciphertext). Decryption reverses this. Authorized users possess a key enabling decryption. The transformation algorithm is called a cipher.

The Caesar Cipher

This historical encryption method, attributed to Julius Caesar, belongs to shift ciphers where letters shift fixed alphabet positions. With a three-position rightward shift: A→D, B→E, wrapping so X→A, Y→B, Z→C.

Plaintext:  ATTACKATDAWN
Ciphertext: DWWDFNDWGDZQ  (shift = 3)

Decryption reverses the process. Though effective historically due to low literacy, modern frequency analysis easily breaks it — a technique developed centuries ago by Arab cryptanalysts.

Historical note: Mary Stuart, Queen of Scots, used similar encryption for the Babington Plot correspondence (16th century), but it was easily deciphered, leading to her conviction and execution.

Polyalphabetic Ciphers

Leon Battista Alberti’s Cipher Disk

Around 1465, Italian polymath Leon Battista Alberti invented the polyalphabetic cipher using two concentric rotating disks. Identical disks belonged to sender and receiver. Rotating the inner disk according to a key letter produced different substitution alphabets within the same message.

Plaintext:  corsodi
Key:        A
Ciphertext: Abeqreuh

After partial encryption, disks rotated with a new key letter (e.g., P) to continue:

Plaintext:  corsodicrittografia
Ciphertext: AbeqreuhPmbsaayqbkpsk

The Vigenère Cipher

Published by Blaise de Vigenère in 1586, this method was long considered unbreakable though weaker than Alberti’s system. It extends Caesar cipher principles using a keyword to vary shifts per letter.

Plaintext:  CORSODICRITTOGRAFIA
Key:        VERMEVERMEVERMEVERME
Ciphertext: ASMGSBOTFORBHSXVLDM

Each key letter determines shift positions. Represented as arithmetic addition modulo 26 (A=0, B=1, etc.), the same plaintext letter encrypts differently based on position, frustrating frequency analysis.

Weakness: Once key length is discovered, the cipher reduces to multiple Caesar ciphers, vulnerable to modern analysis.

The Enigma Machine

The Enigma machine, used by Germans in World War II, represents cryptographic history’s most iconic device. Resembling an electromechanical typewriter, each keypress produced encrypted letters based on rotor and plugboard configurations.

Each rotor functioned like substitution disks; configuration changed dynamically per keystroke. The signal passed through rotor sequences and a reflector reversing the path, doubling complexity.

The first rotor’s complete rotation advanced the second rotor by one position, creating over 17,000 unique states before repeating. Later versions featured four rotors and ten plugboard swaps, exponentially increasing possible configurations.

Encryption and decryption were symmetrical due to the reflector, allowing identical machine configuration for both processes.

Critical weakness: The reflector prevented any letter from encrypting to itself. Combined with recurring military phrases like “Keine besonderen Ereignisse” (“No special events”), cryptanalysts narrowed rotor settings.

Polish and British mathematicians, including Alan Turing at Bletchley Park, achieved breakthrough success. Their work produced Colossus, one of the world’s first large-scale mechanical computers, ultimately cracking Enigma and altering history’s course.

Conclusion

From simple alphabet shifts to sophisticated electromechanical systems, cryptography’s evolution reflects humanity’s continuous quest to secure information against increasingly powerful adversaries.
